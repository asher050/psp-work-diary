Work Diary – Sprint Two (GTA Attendance System)

Day 1 – Client Feedback and Feature Planning
At the start of Sprint Two, we had another meeting with the client to review our progress from Sprint One and understand what improvements were required.
From this discussion, it became clear that while our system worked well as a basic attendance tool, it needed to be expanded to reflect real-world usage more accurately. 
The client introduced additional requirements, particularly around time tracking, reporting, and recording more detailed information about each apprentice’s activity.
One of the first challenges we encountered was related to the database provided by the client. Although we were given access, the database was heavily restricted, 
meaning we could not fully view or use the data as expected. Initially, this created uncertainty within the team, as our plan relied on integrating with this data. 
After discussing possible solutions, we decided to create our own dataset to continue development and ensure we could still demonstrate functionality. 
While this was not ideal, I understood that adapting quickly was more important than waiting for a perfect solution.
During this stage, I contributed to discussions around how we could expand the system. We focused on making the application more realistic rather than just technically functional. 
I found this stage important because it shifted our mindset from “building features” to “building something the client could actually use in practice.”

Day 2 – Implementing Time Tracking and System Logic
On the second day, the focus shifted towards implementing time-based functionality. 
The client required the system to record when an apprentice logs in and out, and to ensure that they meet a six-hour attendance requirement across the day.
This introduced a new level of complexity compared to Sprint One.
Although my main role was front-end development, I was actively involved in how this functionality would work from a user perspective. 
I considered how users would interact with the system and what feedback they would need.
For example, it was important that users clearly understood when their attendance had started and ended, rather than leaving it ambiguous.
One challenge I identified was handling situations where users forget to log out. This made me realise that the system needed to be flexible, not just strict.
As a result, we discussed implementing administrative controls to allow corrections where necessary. 
This demonstrated to me that real-world systems must account for human error rather than assuming perfect usage.

Day 3 – Expanding Features and Improving Usability
On day three, we focused on adding additional features requested by the client. One of these was a daily activity log, where apprentices could record what they had worked on during their session. 
I immediately recognised the value of this feature, as it moved the system beyond simple attendance and towards something that could track progress and productivity.
We also implemented an absence tracking system using specific codes, such as late, sickness, and approved leave. From a design perspective, I understood that this needed to be simple and clear, as too many options or unclear labels could confuse users.
Although the admin dashboard was primarily developed by another team member, I contributed ideas and supported decision-making around how these features should function. I suggested that the system should prioritise clarity and ease of use, ensuring that information could be quickly understood without unnecessary complexity.
At this stage, I felt that the system was becoming more complete and aligned with what the client actually needed, rather than just what we initially assumed.

Day 4 – Admin Functionality and Problem Solving
On day four, the focus was on refining administrative features, particularly the ability to edit attendance records. This was important because we had already identified that users might forget to log in or out, and the system needed to accommodate this.
I worked closely with my teammate by offering suggestions on how these features should behave from a user perspective. For example, I emphasised that any edits should be clear and controlled, rather than allowing unrestricted changes. This made me think more about responsibility within systems, not just functionality.
We also continued working with our custom dataset due to the database limitations. While this allowed us to continue development, I was aware that it did not fully represent real-world conditions. This highlighted a limitation in our project, as we could not fully test how the system would behave with live data.
Despite this, I felt that our ability to adapt and continue progressing demonstrated strong teamwork and problem-solving.

Day 5 – Final Testing, Presentation and Reflection
On the final day, we focused on testing the system and preparing for the client presentation. We went through all features, including time tracking, activity logging, and absence recording, to ensure everything functioned correctly.
While testing, I realised that although the main functionality worked, there were still areas that could be improved, particularly in handling edge cases such as incorrect inputs or missing data. Due to time constraints, we were unable to fully explore all possible scenarios, which I recognised as a limitation of our development process.
During the presentation, I felt confident explaining how the system had evolved since Sprint One. The additional features made the system feel much more complete and realistic. The client responded positively, particularly appreciating the added detail in attendance tracking and reporting.
However, feedback also highlighted that further refinement would be needed for full deployment, especially in terms of integrating with real data and improving reliability. I viewed this feedback positively, as it confirmed that we were moving in the right direction while still leaving room for improvement.
Reflecting on Sprint Two as a whole, I realised that software development is not just about building features, but about adapting to challenges, responding to feedback, and continuously improving the system. I felt that my contribution went beyond my assigned role, as I supported both design decisions and problem-solving throughout the sprint.
