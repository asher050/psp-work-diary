Work Diary – GTA Attendance Registration System

Day 1 – Planning, Research and Design Direction
On the first day, we focused on planning and understanding the purpose of the system before writing any code. I found this stage extremely important because it shaped every decision we made later. 
Instead of jumping straight into development, we discussed the type of users who would be interacting with the system — apprentices working in a car garage environment.
We recognised that the technology available in a garage might be limited. Devices could be older, internet speeds might not be ideal, and users would not have time to navigate complex interfaces. 
Because of this, I pushed for the design to be simple, fast, and very intuitive. My mindset was that if someone can understand it in under 5 seconds, we’ve done it correctly.
I researched colour schemes that would look professional and trustworthy while still feeling modern. We agreed on a dark blue gradient theme, which gives a secure and professional feel. 
I also suggested that consistency across all pages would make the system feel like a real commercial product rather than a student assignment.
Looking back, I’m glad we spent time planning. It prevented confusion later and gave me a clear design standard to follow during development.

Day 2 – Developing the Login Page
On day two, I began developing the login page. My goal was to create something that looked professional and balanced, while also being technically clean and backend-ready.
Initially, I faced some layout challenges. The spacing between the branding section and the form was not aligned properly, and the page did not look as symmetrical as I wanted. 
At first, I felt slightly frustrated because small spacing issues can completely change the feel of a design. However, instead of settling for “good enough,” I reviewed the CSS structure carefully.
I experimented with grid layouts and adjusted padding, alignment, and container widths until the layout felt visually balanced. 
I learned that small changes in spacing (even 5–10 pixels) can significantly affect the professionalism of a page. 
I also ensured the input fields were consistent in size and that the primary “Sign in” button stood out clearly as the main action.
Another issue I encountered was ensuring the page remained responsive. When resizing the browser, some elements didn’t behave as expected. 
I corrected this by using flexible units and responsive layout techniques so that the design adapted smoothly to different screen sizes.
I also made sure the form was structured correctly for backend integration, including proper method types and name attributes. This meant thinking ahead rather than just focusing on appearance.
Overall, I felt very determined during this stage. I didn’t rush the design — I refined it until it genuinely looked like something a professional organisation could use.

Day 3 – Building the Registration (Check-in) Page
On day three, I developed the registration page where students enter a 6-digit code to check in. This page required both visual design and interactive functionality.
One of the first challenges I encountered was designing the six input boxes. Initially, they were either too large or unevenly spaced, which made the page look unprofessional. 
I realised that simply setting fixed sizes was not enough — the boxes needed to scale correctly and fit perfectly within the card container.
To solve this, I adjusted the layout using equal grid columns so that each box automatically resized and aligned evenly. This made the interface look clean and balanced. 
I also refined the spacing until the layout looked visually consistent.
Another issue was managing the user experience when entering the code. At first, users had to manually click into each box, which felt slow and inefficient. 
I implemented logic that automatically moves the cursor to the next box after typing a digit and allows pasting a full code. This significantly improved usability and made the system feel more polished.
I also ensured that the “Check-in” button remains disabled until all six digits are entered. This prevents invalid submissions and makes the interface smarter.
During this process, I tested the API integration and error handling. I made sure the system displayed clear feedback if a code was incorrect and a success message when attendance was recorded.
This day required both patience and attention to detail. I found myself repeatedly testing and adjusting the layout until it matched the professional standard I had in mind. I was highly engaged and motivated to get it right.


Day 4 – Presentation Preparation (Illness and Responsibility)
On day four, we were preparing for our presentation to the client. Unfortunately, I was very ill and unable to attend in person. 
However, I informed my team in advance so they were aware of my situation.
Although I could not be physically present, I made it clear that I was still willing to contribute remotely. 
I told my team that if any final adjustments or fixes were needed, I would complete them from home. 
They appreciated my willingness to remain involved.
At that stage, most of the technical work had been completed, so there were no urgent tasks requiring remote input. 
Even though I was unwell, I ensured communication remained clear and professional.
This experience showed me the importance of responsibility and teamwork in group projects.

Day 5 – Final Testing, Client Presentation and Feedback
On the final day, we met to complete final testing and prepare for our client presentation. Before presenting, we carefully went through the entire website to ensure everything was functioning correctly. 
I reviewed the login page and the registration page to confirm that the layout remained consistent, the API integration worked as expected, and that error and success messages displayed properly. 
We also checked responsiveness to make sure the system looked professional on different screen sizes.
Since we were presenting to a client, I understood the importance of demonstrating not just that the system worked, but that we had thought carefully about design, usability, and future scalability. 
We rehearsed what each of us would say and made sure we clearly explained why we made certain design decisions — especially why we prioritised simplicity for apprentices working in a garage environment.
During the presentation, I felt confident discussing the parts of the system I had developed. I explained how the login page was designed to look professional and secure, and how the 6-digit registration code system was built to be fast and user-friendly. 
The client responded very positively to our progress so far and was clearly impressed with how professional the interface looked at this stage.
However, we also received valuable feedback. The client suggested potential improvements for future development, including refining certain features, considering additional functionality for tracking attendance history more clearly, and improving certain small interaction elements.
There were also suggestions about how the system could be adapted in the future to scale as more apprentices join.
While the feedback highlighted areas for improvement, I saw this as constructive rather than negative. It showed that the client was genuinely engaged and interested in the future of the system.
I took responsibility for writing detailed notes during the feedback session so I could share them clearly with my teammates afterwards. 
I wanted to ensure that we did not miss any important suggestions and that we could implement improvements effectively in the next phase.
Reflecting on this day, I realised how important communication and professionalism are when presenting technical work. It is not just about writing code — it is about explaining decisions, receiving feedback positively, and showing willingness to improve. 
I felt proud of how we presented ourselves as a team and how we handled feedback maturely and constructively.
